🔵 Part 1: Initial Assessment — Every Patient, Every Time
📞 Opening Script

"Hello, this is [Name], the triage nurse at [Practice Name]. I understand you're calling about [chief complaint]. Before we discuss your symptoms, I'd like to ask a few questions to make sure we provide the best care for you."

Step 1: Review EMR Before Proceeding: Pull Up EMR
  • Medication list
  • Clinical summary
  • Last visit note
  • Confirm allergies
  • Look for any recent labs
Step 2: Basic Screening Questions (All Chief Complaints)
  • When did it start?
  • Has it happened before?
  • Duration — how long?
  • Anything that makes it better or worse?
  • Have you taken or tried anything to make it better?
  • What medications or ekits do you have in the home?
  • "Can you (or your caregiver) obtain any vital signs for me?"
  • Heart rate
  • Blood pressure
  • Oxygen saturation (pulse oximeter)
  • Blood glucose (if diabetic)
Step 3: Goals of Care Assessment
  • Code status (from EMR)
  • Preference to avoid the ED if possible?

🟣 If You Think a Hospice Transition May Be Appropriate

Step 1 — Assess Understanding
"Tell me what you understand about your disease."
"What do you think this change/worsening means for your disease progression?"
Step 2 — Assess Goals
"What is most important to you right now?"
"What are you most worried about right now?"
Step 3 — Assess Needs
Are you able to get around your house, cook, bathe (ADLs)?
Would it be helpful to have someone come a few times a week?
"What is the one thing that if we could improve it would make things better?"
Step 4 — Hope / Worry / Fear Statement
"I very much hope that you will recover from this episode, but I worry that this is a worsening of your disease, and I fear that if we don't move to a higher level of care, we won't be able to control your symptoms, get you the support you need, and keep you at home."
Step 5 — Introduce Hospice
"From what you've said, it sounds like it would be useful to have a [nurse to see you more often / person to help with baths / additional spiritual and social support / respite for your family]."
"The best way to get you these services is with hospice."
Step 6 — Recommend Hospice
"Given that you would like to avoid the hospital and focus on your family, and that your needs are increasing, I think hospice is the best solution to meet your needs."
ℹ️ If patient declines → continue with symptom triage questions and direct to NP.
🫁 Dyspnea (Shortness of Breath)
Triage Assessment (Complete BEFORE branching)
  • When did it start? (Acute vs. Gradual)
  • Has it happened before?
  • Anything that makes it better or worse?
  • Have you taken or tried anything?
  • What medications or ekits in the home?
  • Can you obtain vital signs?
  • Do you have a pulse oximeter at home? Can you check your oxygen saturation?
⚡ ACUTE Onset ▶
  • Chest pain? → Chest Pain
  • Swelling in lower extremities or abdomen? → Edema
  • Recent procedures — pacemaker, biopsy, bronchoscopy, cardiac stents, surgery?
  • Previous ICU stay or breathing tube?
  • What is the heart rate? (bradycardic/tachycardic) Can they take their pulse?
  • Cough? → Cough
  • Recent fall? → Falls
  • New medications or increased doses?
  • ASA?
  • Have you been in bed more recently? Why?
  • Recent social or health changes that are stressful?
📈 GRADUAL Onset ▶
  • Over how many days?
  • Fever?
  • Cough?
  • Sputum production?
  • Blood? — New or chronic? How much?
  • Chest pain? → Chest Pain
  • Swelling in lower extremities or abdomen? → Edema
  • Medication compliance?
  • Diet — sodium and fluid intake?
  • Recent trouble swallowing?
  • If diabetes — blood sugar consistently > 450?
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Severe respiratory distress with inability to speak
  • Blue lips or fingertips (cyanosis)
  • Altered mental status with respiratory distress
  • Suspected choking or airway obstruction
  • New-onset stridor
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"If your goal is to be comfortable at home rather than go to the hospital, we can arrange for hospice to provide medications and support to help with your breathing. Would you like me to discuss this option with the doctor?"

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • New or worsening dyspnea at rest
  • Dyspnea with fever (concern for pneumonia)
  • Dyspnea with leg swelling (concern for HF or DVT/PE)
  • O₂ sat ≤90% (if home pulse oximeter available)
  • Not responding to current home O₂ or medications
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "Sit upright or in a comfortable position"
  • "Use a fan directed at your face — this can help reduce breathlessness"
  • "Open a window or lower room temperature if possible"
  • "Use your oxygen as prescribed"
  • "Take slow, deep breaths — in through your nose, out through pursed lips"
  • "If you have a prescribed as-needed medication for breathing, you may take it now"
🦵 Edema
Triage Questions
  • When did it start?
  • Have you done anything to make it better or worse?
  • Bilateral or Unilateral?
  • When you press on your legs, does it leave an indention?
↔️ BILATERAL Edema ▶
  • Has this happened before?
  • Hx of CHF, cirrhosis, CKD, afib?
  • Medication compliance?
  • What medications at home?
  • Diet changes?
  • Weight gain above dry weight?
📋 Obtain exact history on medications taken, diet changes, and weight changes.
  • If this has NOT happened before — any hx of heart disease, alcohol use, hepatitis, HTN, or diabetes?
If CHF ▶
  • Dyspnea or cough?
  • Worse when lying down?
  • Sleeping in a chair or on pillows?
  • Did it come on quickly?
If Cirrhosis ▶
  • Abdominal distension?
  • Swelling "all over?"
If CKD ▶
  • Changes in urination?
  • Decreased frequency?
  • Change in color?
  • Frothy or bubbly urine?
  • Swelling around eyes or face?
💊
Flag these meds if on the list:
pioglitazone rosiglitazone amlodipine nifedipine terazosin prazosin doxazosin minoxidil hydralazine NSAIDs
→ UNILATERAL Edema ▶
  • Did it come on quickly?
  • Assess DVT risk factors:
  • Cancer?
  • Immobility?
  • Recent surgery?
  • Previous hx of DVT/PE?
  • Pain with standing on foot and leaning forward?
  • Non-pitting (no indention)? → Assess for lymphedema:
  • Breast surgery?
  • Vascular surgery?
  • Significant obesity?
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Sudden severe swelling with shortness of breath (anaphylaxis or acute HF)
  • Unilateral leg swelling with chest pain or SOB (PE)
  • Facial/throat swelling with difficulty breathing
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"If your swelling is related to your heart or kidney condition and you prefer to focus on comfort, hospice can help manage this at home with medications. Would you like to discuss this?"

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • New unilateral leg swelling (concern for DVT)
  • Bilateral swelling with worsening SOB
  • Swelling with fever or redness (cellulitis)
  • Rapid weight gain (>3 lbs/24h or >5 lbs/week)
  • Decreased urine output with increasing swelling
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "Elevate your legs above heart level when sitting or lying down"
  • "Limit salt intake"
  • "Take your diuretic (water pill) as prescribed"
  • "Weigh yourself daily and call if you gain more than 3 pounds overnight"
  • "Wear compression stockings if you have them and can tolerate them"
💨 Cough
Triage Questions
  • When did it start?
  • Does anything make it better or worse?
  • Have you taken any medications for it?
  • What medications or ekits do you have at home?
  • Do you have a pulse oximeter at home? Can you check your oxygen saturation?
⚡ ACUTE — Less than 3 weeks ▶
  • Wheezing? Better with inhalers?
  • LE edema or weight gain?
  • Fever? → Fever
  • Sputum production? Has it changed?
  • Blood?
  • Chest pain? → Chest Pain
📈 CHRONIC — More than 3 weeks ▶
📅 Can be seen non-urgently by NP or make PCP appointment.
  • Sinus drainage? Worse at night? Post-nasal drip?
  • Reflux symptoms / GERD?
  • Other more complicated cause requiring PCP or specialist workup?
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Coughing up large amounts of blood (hemoptysis)
  • Severe respiratory distress with cough
  • Choking or inability to clear airway
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"If your cough is related to your underlying condition and you prefer comfort care, hospice can provide medications to suppress the cough and keep you comfortable at home."

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • Cough with fever >100.4°F
  • Cough with new or worsening SOB
  • Blood-tinged or rust-colored sputum
  • Cough with chest pain
  • Cough preventing sleep or eating
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "Stay hydrated with warm fluids like tea or broth"
  • "Use a humidifier if available"
  • "Elevate your head when lying down"
  • "Honey (1–2 tsp) may help soothe cough if you can swallow safely"
  • "OTC cough suppressants may help if you have them"
❤️‍🩹 Chest Pain
Detailed Assessment
  • Duration — how long?
  • Location & radiation — side of chest? Center of chest?
  • Quality — ripping, tight, sharp?
  • Reproducible? — If you press on it, does it elicit the pain?
  • Severity:
  • Sharp and stabbing
  • Tearing
  • Dull and aching
  • Pressure/heaviness ("elephant on your chest")
  • Tightness
  • Burning
Associated Symptoms — Screen All
  • Better with rest?
  • N/V/diaphoresis?
  • Better with nitro?
  • Syncope?
  • Worse with breathing in (pleuritic)?
  • Within days of MI?
  • Cough?
  • Dyspnea?
  • Fatigue?
  • Worse when leaning forward?
  • Fever?
  • Wheezing?
  • Worse at night?
  • Anxiety?
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Chest pain with sweating, nausea, or SOB
  • Radiating to arm, jaw, neck, or back
  • Severe sudden-onset chest pain
  • Tearing/ripping pain radiating to back (aortic dissection)
  • Chest pain with loss of consciousness
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"If you have a known heart condition and your goal is comfort rather than hospitalization, we can arrange for hospice to manage your symptoms at home. This includes medications for pain and anxiety. Would you like to discuss this with the doctor?"

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • Chest pain that is new or different from usual
  • Chest pain with palpitations
  • Chest pain with fever (pericarditis, pneumonia)
  • Not relieved by rest or nitroglycerin (if prescribed)
  • History of heart disease with any chest discomfort
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "Rest in a comfortable position"
  • "If you have nitroglycerin prescribed, take it as directed"
  • "Chew an aspirin (325 mg) if not allergic and no contraindications"
  • "Loosen any tight clothing"
  • "Stay calm and avoid exertion"
💪 Generalized Weakness
🚨 Change in mental status? → Notify NP URGENTLY
Key Differentiator
  • Objective muscle weakness or malaise?
  • Ask: "Do you feel you really could not lift a book or get out of a chair if you tried and wanted to, versus just feeling weaker and more tired all over?"
💪 OBJECTIVE Muscle Weakness ▶
  • Visual field or speech disturbances?
  • Decreased respiratory status?
  • Known cancer with mets to spine?
  • Incontinence of bowel or bladder?
  • Concern for or history of seizures?
  • Recent tick bite?
😴 MALAISE (Generalized fatigue/tiredness) ▶
  • Fever or infection risk? → Fever
  • Chest pain or concern for MI? → Chest Pain
  • Check blood sugar
🩸 If blood sugar low → advise juice and repeat BG
  • Medication changes? What are they?
  • Recent weight loss? How much?
  • Eating and drinking normally?
  • Normal urine output?
  • History of seizures?
  • Hx of neuromuscular disorders (MS)?
  • Risk for carbon monoxide poisoning? (heaters in winter)
🚨 If CO poisoning risk → Notify NP URGENTLY
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Sudden severe weakness with altered mental status
  • Weakness with chest pain or SOB
  • Weakness with signs of shock (pale, clammy, rapid pulse)
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"Progressive weakness is often part of the natural course of serious illness. If your goal is to remain comfortable at home, hospice can provide support with daily activities and symptom management."

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • New-onset weakness (acute change from baseline)
  • Weakness with fever
  • Weakness with decreased oral intake or dehydration
  • Weakness after a fall
  • Weakness with new pain
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "Rest in bed or a comfortable chair"
  • "Ensure adequate hydration — small sips of fluids frequently"
  • "Eat small, frequent meals if able"
  • "Use assistive devices (walker, cane) when moving"
  • "Do not attempt to walk alone if feeling unsteady"
⚡ Unilateral Weakness (Concern for Stroke)
⚠️ NOTE: Call 911 regardless of goals of care if acute onset <24 hours. Hospice transition only appropriate for known stroke history or symptoms >24h and stable.
Assessment
  • When did it start?
  • Has it happened before?
  • Duration?
  • Slow progression — has it gotten progressively worse or any better?
  • Acute — headache?
  • Anything makes it better or worse?
  • Tried anything to make it better?
  • Medications or ekits in the home?
  • Vital signs available?
Associated Symptoms
  • Slurred speech?
  • Is the face involved?
  • Change in vision?
  • Loss of consciousness?
  • On anticoagulation?
  • Hx of strokes, heart attacks, PVD, DM, renal disease, HTN?
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Sudden weakness or numbness on one side of face, arm, or leg
  • Sudden confusion or trouble speaking
  • Sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes
  • Sudden trouble walking, dizziness, or loss of balance
  • Sudden severe headache with no known cause
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"If you've had a stroke before and your goal is comfort care, hospice can help manage your symptoms at home. Let me discuss this with the doctor."

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • Symptoms resolved but occurred within past 24 hours (TIA)
  • Gradual onset weakness (not sudden)
  • Known history of stroke with similar symptoms to baseline
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "FAST Assessment:"
  • "Face: Smile for me. Does one side droop?"
  • "Arms: Raise both arms. Does one drift?"
  • "Speech: Repeat: The sky is blue. Is speech slurred?"
  • "Time: When did symptoms start?"

🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"Confusion and agitation can occur as part of serious illness. If your goal is comfort, hospice can provide medications and support to keep your loved one calm and comfortable at home."

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • New confusion or change from baseline
  • Confusion with fever
  • Confusion with new medications
  • Confusion with decreased urine output (uremia)
  • Confusion with constipation
  • Agitation not controlled with current medications
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "Keep the environment calm and quiet"
  • "Reduce stimulation — dim lights, turn off TV"
  • "Speak in a calm, reassuring voice"
  • "Reorient gently: You're at home. I'm [name]. You're safe."
  • "Ensure safety — remove sharp objects, prevent falls"
  • "Check for reversible causes: constipation, urinary retention, pain, dehydration"
🌡️ Fever
🚨
IMMEDIATE ESCALATION if any:
• Cancer + concern for neutropenia → assess goals, urgently notify NP or send to ED
• Low blood pressure → Notify NP IMMEDIATELY
• Change in mental status → Notify NP IMMEDIATELY
• ASA or Tylenol overdose concern → Notify NP IMMEDIATELY
💊
On immunosuppressants? Steroids, immunomodulators, transplant drugs → Notify NP URGENTLY
Assessment
  • When did it start? Highest temperature? Getting lower?
  • Have you taken anything for it?
  • Any sick contacts? When, and what did they have?
Evaluate for Infections
  • Lung — cough, sputum
  • Urine — dysuria, frequency
  • Skin — erythema, purulence
  • Abdomen/GI — diarrhea, pain
Medication Considerations
  • New medications? SSRI TCA MAO
  • Recent vaccinations?
  • Concern for ASA or Tylenol overdose?
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Fever with AMS and stiff neck (meningitis)
  • Fever with severe respiratory distress
  • Fever with signs of shock (pale, clammy, rapid pulse, low BP)
  • Fever >104°F with confusion
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"Fever is common in serious illness and doesn't always need aggressive treatment. If comfort is the goal, we can manage fever symptoms at home with medications. Antibiotics may or may not help depending on the cause."

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • Fever >100.4°F with new symptoms (cough, dysuria, wound changes)
  • Fever in immunocompromised patient
  • Fever not responding to acetaminophen
  • Fever with chills or rigors
  • Fever with indwelling catheter or other device
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "Take acetaminophen (Tylenol) as directed — usually 650–1000 mg every 6 hours"
  • "Stay hydrated with cool fluids"
  • "Use light clothing and blankets"
  • "A cool washcloth on the forehead may provide comfort"
  • "Monitor temperature every 4–6 hours"

Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Head injury with loss of consciousness
  • Inability to move a limb (possible fracture)
  • Severe pain preventing movement
  • Visible deformity of limb or joint
  • Anticoagulant use with head strike
  • Unable to get up from floor
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"Falls can be frightening. If your goal is comfort care and there's no obvious serious injury, hospice can evaluate you at home and help with pain management and safety planning."

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • Head strike (even without LOC), especially on blood thinners
  • New pain but able to move all limbs
  • Syncope or near-syncope preceding the fall
  • Recurrent falls
  • Prolonged time on floor (>1 hour) — concern for rhabdomyolysis
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "If on the floor and not injured, get up slowly using a sturdy chair"
  • "If you cannot get up, stay calm and call for help"
  • "Apply ice to painful areas for 15–20 minutes"
  • "Take acetaminophen for pain if needed"
  • "Do not walk alone until you've been evaluated"
🩺 Pain
Assessment
  • Has it happened before?
  • Duration?
  • For new pain — acute or recent event?
  • For old pain — changes in medications or events recently?
  • Anything makes it better or worse?
  • Tried anything to make it better?
  • Location and radiation?
  • Quality — ripping, tight, sharp, etc.?
  • Severity?
  • Associated symptoms?
  • What medications available at home?
Cross-References Based on Findings
  • Recent falls? → Falls
  • Chest pain? → Chest Pain
  • Shortness of breath? → Dyspnea
  • Abdominal pain? → Abdominal Pain
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Severe sudden-onset pain (worst pain of life)
  • Pain with signs of shock
  • Pain with neurologic symptoms (weakness, numbness, incontinence)
  • Severe abdominal pain with rigid abdomen
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"Pain management is a priority in hospice care. If comfort is your goal, hospice can provide strong pain medications at home, including around-the-clock dosing and breakthrough medications."

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • Pain not controlled by current medications
  • New pain or change in pain pattern
  • Pain with fever
  • Pain with functional decline
  • Pain requiring dose adjustment
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "Take your pain medication as prescribed"
  • "If you have breakthrough pain medication, you may take it now"
  • "Try positioning changes for comfort"
  • "Apply heat or ice to the painful area if appropriate"
  • "Use relaxation techniques: slow deep breathing, distraction"
🔶 Constipation
Triage Questions
  • Last BM?
  • Have you taken or tried anything to make it better?
  • What laxatives do you have in the home?
  • Was it hard or soft?
Rectal Bleeding Screen
✅ Bright red on outside of stool / spots on toilet paper → concern for hemorrhoids or anal fissure, provide reassurance.
🚨 Blood IN stool → Notify NP urgently — is HR elevated or BP low?
🚨 Black tarry stool → Notify NP urgently — is HR elevated or BP low?
Abdominal Distension? ▶
  • Over what period of time?
  • Acute — Hx of Crohn's, UC, recent surgery, colon or ovarian cancer?
  • Over days to weeks — weight gain? Hx of cirrhosis, CHF, or renal disease?
Medication Review
  • Check for constipating medications:
  • Opioids Scopolamine Benadryl Phenergan Amitriptyline Nortriptyline Seroquel Zyprexa
  • Iron Amlodipine Nifedipine Verapamil NSAIDs Diuretics
Additional History
  • Diabetes — history of gastroparesis?
  • Neuromuscular disorders — Parkinson's, MS, ALS, spinal cord injury?
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Severe abdominal pain with vomiting (bowel obstruction)
  • Abdominal distension with inability to pass gas
  • Fever with severe abdominal tenderness
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"Constipation is very common, especially with pain medications. Hospice can help manage this with a bowel regimen and, if needed, more intensive treatments at home."

📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • No BM in >3 days despite laxatives
  • Constipation with nausea and vomiting
  • Constipation with abdominal distension
  • Overflow diarrhea (liquid stool around impaction)
  • New or worsening abdominal pain
☎ RN Telephone Management — Scripts ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "Increase fluid intake if able"
  • "Take laxatives as prescribed — usually senna 2 tablets twice daily"
  • "You may add MiraLAX 17g in 8 oz water daily"
  • "A glycerin suppository may help if no BM in 2–3 days"
  • "Gentle clockwise abdominal massage may help"
  • "Sit upright on toilet with feet on a stool if possible"

💓 Arrhythmia
Immediate RN Assessment

🟣 Script

⬆️ Hypertension
Immediate RN Assessment

🟣 Script

  • No acute end-organ damage

📞 Patient Instructions (Elevated BP Without Severe Symptoms)

⬇️ Hypotension
Immediate RN Assessment
  • Instruct patient to lie down immediately with legs elevated 12 inches if tolerated
  • If standing or sitting, assist to safe lying position to prevent falls → Falls
  • Assess symptoms: dizziness, lightheadedness, weakness, nausea, blurred vision, confusion → AMS
  • Check for orthostatic changes if able: BP lying, then after standing 1–2 minutes (drop ≥20 mmHg SBP or ≥10 mmHg DBP = orthostatic)
  • Review recent events: medication changes, vomiting, diarrhea → Diarrhea, decreased oral intake, prolonged standing, hot environment
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • SBP <90 mmHg with altered mental status, chest pain, or severe dyspnea
  • Signs of shock (cold/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, confusion)
  • Suspected sepsis (fever, hypotension, AMS) → Fever
  • Syncope with ongoing altered mental status

🟣 Script

  • Hospice team available for assessment

  • New hypotension with medication change

🍯 Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar)
⚡
TREAT FIRST, THEN CALL PROVIDER. Do not wait to begin treatment while awaiting callback.
Immediate RN Assessment
  • Confirm glucose value, time of last meal, and last insulin/sulfonylurea dose
  • Assess for symptoms: shakiness, sweating, confusion → AMS, irritability, rapid heartbeat, lightheadedness
  • Can patient swallow safely? Any recent falls? → Falls
  • Is glucagon available in the home and is a caregiver trained?
The Rule of 15 — Give 15 g Fast-Acting Carbohydrate
  • 4 oz (½ cup) fruit juice or regular soda
  • 3–4 glucose tablets
  • 1 tablespoon honey or sugar
  • 6–8 hard candies
🔁
Recheck in 15 minutes. If still <70 mg/dL, repeat with another 15 g. Once >70 mg/dL, give a small snack with protein (crackers with peanut butter, cheese and crackers).
If Patient Cannot Swallow or Is Unconscious
  • If glucagon available and caregiver trained — administer glucagon per kit instructions
  • Position patient on side to prevent aspiration
  • Do NOT give food or fluids by mouth
  • Call 911 immediately
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • BG <50 mg/dL with AMS, seizure, or loss of consciousness
  • Unable to treat orally and no caregiver trained in glucagon
  • Persistent hypoglycemia despite oral treatment
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"In end-stage disease with poor oral intake, repeat lows are often a sign that diabetes medications need to be reduced. Hospice can manage this at home with glucose gel and medication deintensification."

  • Recurrent hypoglycemia in end-stage disease with poor oral intake
  • Goals of care focus on comfort; family/hospice can manage with glucose gel or small amounts of juice
  • Medication deintensification already planned
📞 Escalate to Provider Immediately IF ▶
  • BG <70 mg/dL (treat first, then call)
  • BG 70–100 mg/dL with symptoms or recurrent pattern
  • Hypoglycemia in patient on insulin or sulfonylureas requiring med adjustment
  • Any hypoglycemia in frail elderly or cognitive impairment
☎ RN Telephone Management — After Treatment ▶
📞 Patient Instructions
  • "Rest for 30–60 minutes after your glucose is back to normal"
  • "No driving or operating machinery for at least 1 hour"
  • "Check your blood glucose every 1–2 hours for the next 4–6 hours"
  • "Eat your regular meals and snacks as scheduled"
  • "Try to identify the trigger — skipped meal, extra exercise, alcohol — and avoid it"
  • "Carry fast-acting carbohydrate with you at all times"
📋

  • Review recent fluid intake and urine output
  • Suggest family obtain and use an over-the-counter dipstick to test the urine if possible
Critical Distinction — Symptomatic UTI vs. Asymptomatic Bacteriuria
⚠️
Symptomatic UTI requires ≥2 of: fever, new/worsened urinary urgency or frequency, acute dysuria, suprapubic tenderness, costovertebral angle pain or tenderness.

Asymptomatic bacteriuria: positive urine culture without localizing GU symptoms or systemic signs of infection.

In older adults with cognitive impairment: confusion or delirium alone WITHOUT fever or localizing urinary symptoms does NOT indicate UTI and should NOT be treated with antibiotics.
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Positive UA with signs of sepsis: fever >101°F, hypotension (SBP <90) → Hypotension, AMS, rigors
  • Suspected pyelonephritis with severe symptoms: high fever, severe flank pain, persistent vomiting, inability to take oral fluids
  • Gross hematuria with hemodynamic instability or urinary retention
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"UTIs can often be managed at home with antibiotics and comfort medications, without a hospital trip. If avoiding the ED matters to you, hospice can provide this care at home."

  • Symptoms manageable with comfort medications (antibiotics if available through hospice, pain management, antiemetics)
  • Hospice nurse available for urgent home visit
  • Family/caregiver comfortable with symptom management at current location
📞 Escalate to Provider IF ▶
  • Positive UA with UTI symptoms: dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain, fever ≤101°F
  • Asymptomatic bacteriuria in catheterized patient with clinical change (new confusion, functional decline, fever)
  • Gross hematuria without hemodynamic instability
  • Pyuria with symptoms in patient at risk for complicated UTI (diabetes, immunosuppression, recent urologic procedure)
  • Positive UA in male patient (all men require urine culture before treatment)
  • Uncertainty about goals of care or appropriateness of antibiotic treatment
☎ RN Telephone Management — Home Instructions ▶
📞 Comfort Measures Pending Provider Callback
  • "Drink more fluids — aim for 8–10 glasses of water a day if it's safe for you"
  • "Urinate every 2–3 hours while awake — don't hold it"
  • "A heating pad on your lower abdomen or back for 15–20 minutes can help"
  • "Avoid bladder irritants: caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods, artificial sweeteners, acidic foods"
  • "If you have Pyridium from before, it can help with the burning — your urine will turn orange"
📞 For Catheterized Patients
  • "Make sure the bag is below bladder level at all times"
  • "Empty the bag every 8 hours or when it's two-thirds full"
  • "Keep the catheter and surrounding area clean with soap and water"
  • "Call back immediately if the catheter is leaking, blocked, or dislodged"
⚠️
Do NOT treat asymptomatic bacteriuria in catheterized patients unless fever or systemic symptoms present.
🟣
Palliative/Hospice: For actively dying patients, antibiotics may not improve comfort and may prolong dying. Bacteriuria is common in advanced illness and does not always require treatment. Treat only if symptomatic and the patient has weeks to months of life expectancy and desires symptom relief.
📋
Documentation: UA values (bacteria, WBCs, nitrites, leukocyte esterase, blood, protein), symptoms and duration, vitals if available, catheter/urologic history, current meds, recurrent UTI/resistance history, goals of care, teach-back confirmation.
When to Call Back — Patient/Caregiver Education
  • Fever >101°F or worsening fever
  • Severe back or flank pain
  • Nausea, vomiting, inability to keep fluids down
  • Blood in urine (new or worsening)
  • Confusion or altered mental status (new or worsening)
  • No improvement after 48 hours of antibiotics
  • Inability to urinate or severe urinary retention
🩸 Anemia (Low Hemoglobin)
Immediate RN Assessment
  • Assess for symptoms: fatigue, weakness, shortness of breath → Dyspnea, chest pain → Chest Pain, dizziness, palpitations, pale skin/mucous membranes
  • Ask about recent bleeding: menstrual (if applicable), GI (black tarry stools, bright red blood, vomiting blood), urinary, nosebleeds, bruising
  • Review recent activity tolerance and changes from baseline
  • Assess fall risk → Falls
  • Check BP and HR if available
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Hgb <7 g/dL with symptoms: chest pain, severe dyspnea, AMS, syncope
  • Hgb <6 g/dL regardless of symptoms
  • Signs of active severe bleeding: hematemesis, melena, bright red blood per rectum, severe epistaxis
  • Hemodynamic instability: hypotension → Hypotension, tachycardia, AMS → AMS
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"Transfusions give temporary relief for a few days to weeks, but require a trip to the hospital or infusion center. Hospice can focus on managing the fatigue and breathing symptoms at home with oxygen and activity modification. Would you like to talk about this option?"

  • Patient enrolled in hospice with expected anemia from underlying disease
  • Goals of care prioritize comfort over transfusion
  • Symptoms manageable with oxygen, rest, activity modification
📞 Escalate to Provider Immediately IF ▶
  • Hgb 7–8 g/dL with mild to moderate symptoms
  • Hgb <7 g/dL in asymptomatic patient
  • New anemia (drop ≥2 g/dL from baseline) with symptoms
  • Evidence of ongoing bleeding without hemodynamic instability
  • Anemia requiring transfusion consideration in context of goals of care
☎ RN Telephone Management — Home Instructions ▶
📞 Patient Instructions (Mild to Moderate Anemia)
  • "Rest and avoid strenuous activity"
  • "Rise slowly from sitting or lying down"
  • "Sit or lie down immediately if you feel dizzy or lightheaded"
  • "If you eat meat, iron-rich foods help: red meat, poultry, fish, beans, lentils, fortified cereals, dark leafy greens"
  • "Take iron supplements with orange juice (vitamin C) if prescribed"
  • "Don't take tea, coffee, or calcium within 2 hours of iron — they block absorption"
  • "Check your stool color — black/tarry = upper GI bleeding, bright red = lower GI bleeding; call back either way"
📞 Activity Modifications
  • "Avoid driving or operating machinery if dizzy"
  • "Use assistive devices to prevent falls"
  • "Pace yourself — alternate activity with rest"
  • "Avoid hot showers or baths — they can make dizziness worse"
🟣
Palliative/Hospice: Focus on energy conservation, oxygen for dyspnea (typically for SpO₂ <90%), and reassurance about expected fatigue in advanced illness.
💧 Thrombocytopenia (Low Platelets)
Immediate RN Assessment
  • Assess for signs of bleeding: petechiae (pinpoint red/purple spots), purpura, bruising, nosebleeds, gum bleeding, blood in urine or stool, heavy menstrual bleeding
  • Review medications affecting platelets: aspirin, NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen), clopidogrel, warfarin → INR, heparin, certain antibiotics, chemotherapy
  • Assess recent trauma, procedures, or falls → Falls
  • Check for signs of serious bleeding: severe headache, vision changes, weakness, confusion → AMS, severe abdominal pain → Abd Pain
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • Platelets <10,000/μL with active bleeding (any site)
  • Platelets <20,000/μL with major bleeding: intracranial, GI, GU, pulmonary
  • Signs of intracranial bleeding: severe headache, vision changes, weakness, seizures, AMS
  • Hemodynamic instability with bleeding
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"Platelet transfusions give temporary benefit — usually 2–4 days — and require hospital visits. Hospice can focus on bleeding prevention and comfort at home. Would that fit your goals?"

  • Patient enrolled in hospice with expected thrombocytopenia (bone marrow failure, malignancy)
  • Goals of care prioritize comfort over platelet transfusion
  • Bleeding manageable with local measures and comfort care
📞 Escalate to Provider Immediately IF ▶
  • Platelets <50,000/μL regardless of symptoms
  • Platelets 50,000–100,000/μL with bleeding (petechiae, bruising, nosebleeds, gum bleeding)
  • Any platelet count with signs of bleeding requiring intervention
  • Patient on antiplatelet or anticoagulant medications
☎ RN Telephone Management — Bleeding Precautions ▶
📞 Personal Care
  • "Use a soft toothbrush and brush gently"
  • "Don't floss if platelets are under 20,000"
  • "Use an electric razor — not a blade"
  • "File your nails instead of cutting them"
  • "Blow your nose gently — not forcefully"
  • "Avoid rectal thermometers, suppositories, and enemas"
📞 Medication & Activity
  • "Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs — ibuprofen, naproxen, ketorolac"
  • "Use acetaminophen (Tylenol) for pain instead"
  • "Check all OTC medications — many contain aspirin or NSAIDs"
  • "Avoid contact sports and activities with injury risk"
  • "Wear protective footwear — don't go barefoot"
  • "Avoid constipation — straining can cause bleeding; use stool softeners, fluids, fiber" → Constipation
📞 Managing Bleeding at Home
  • "For minor cuts — direct pressure for 10–15 minutes without peeking"
  • "For nosebleeds — sit upright, lean forward, pinch the soft part for 10–15 minutes; ice on the bridge of the nose"
  • "For gum bleeding — pressure with gauze or a tea bag (the tannin helps clotting)"
  • "For bruising — ice pack for 15–20 minutes"
⚠️
Teach warning signs for 911: severe headache, vision changes, weakness, black tarry stools, vomiting blood, severe abdominal pain.
🛡️ Leukopenia / Neutropenia (Low WBC)
🚨
Febrile neutropenia is an oncologic emergency. Any fever (>100.4°F) in a patient with ANC <1,000/μL requires immediate provider contact.
Immediate RN Assessment
  • Assess for signs of infection: fever → Fever, chills, cough → Cough, sore throat, urinary symptoms → UTI, skin infections, mouth sores
  • Take temperature if available — fever in a neutropenic patient is an emergency
  • Recent exposures to illness or sick contacts?
  • Is patient on chemotherapy, immunosuppressive medications, or does patient have underlying immunodeficiency?
  • Calculate ANC if available: ANC = WBC × (% neutrophils + % bands) ÷ 100
Disposition & Management
🚨 CALL 911 IF (and patient/family desires aggressive intervention) ▶
  • ANC <500/μL with fever >100.4°F (38°C) = febrile neutropenia
  • Signs of sepsis: fever, hypotension, AMS, rigors, severe illness
  • Neutropenia with signs of severe infection: pneumonia, cellulitis, abscess
🏠 Hospice Transition Alternative ▶
🟣 Script

"For advanced illness, we can manage fevers and infections at home with oral antibiotics and comfort medications — rather than admitting you for IV antibiotics. Would that fit your goals?"

  • Patient enrolled in hospice with expected neutropenia from underlying disease or chemotherapy
  • Goals of care prioritize comfort over aggressive infection treatment
  • Symptoms manageable with oral antibiotics and comfort care through hospice
📞 Escalate to Provider Immediately IF ▶
  • ANC <1,000/μL with any signs of infection (fever, cough, sore throat, urinary symptoms, skin infection)
  • ANC <500/μL even without fever (high risk for rapid deterioration)
  • Neutropenia in patient on chemotherapy or immunosuppressive medications
  • Any fever in a neutropenic patient
  • Uncertainty about goals of care or appropriateness of antibiotic prophylaxis
☎ RN Telephone Management — Neutropenic Precautions ▶
📞 Infection Prevention
  • "Avoid crowds and people who are sick — including children with colds"
  • "Wash hands frequently with soap and water for at least 20 seconds"
  • "Wear a mask in public if your ANC is under 500"
  • "Avoid anyone who got a live vaccine in the last 2 weeks (chickenpox, MMR, nasal flu)"
📞 Food Safety
  • "No raw or undercooked eggs, meat, fish, or shellfish"
  • "No unpasteurized dairy, juices, or honey"
  • "Wash and peel fruits and vegetables"
  • "No deli meats or soft cheeses unless heated until steaming"
  • "Avoid buffets, salad bars, and street vendors"
📞 Environmental & Personal Hygiene
  • "Avoid gardening and contact with soil, plants, fresh flowers (fungal spores)"
  • "Don't clean litter boxes, bird cages, or fish tanks"
  • "Shower or bathe daily; keep skin moisturized to prevent cracks"
  • "Brush teeth gently with a soft toothbrush after meals"
  • "Don't cut cuticles — push back gently"
  • "Report any skin breaks, cuts, or rashes immediately"
📞 Temperature Monitoring
  • "Check your temperature twice daily if your ANC is under 1,000"
  • "Check immediately if you feel feverish, chilled, or unwell"
  • "Fever = 100.4°F (38°C) once, or 100°F (37.8°C) sustained over an hour"
  • "Call us right away for any fever, chills, new cough, shortness of breath, severe sore throat, mouth sores, diarrhea, painful urination, or skin redness/drainage"
🟣
Palliative/Hospice: Modified precautions — focus on hand hygiene and avoiding obviously sick contacts, but allow meaningful activities (gardening, pet contact) if important to the patient. For actively dying patients, precautions may be relaxed to allow family contact and meaningful activities.
